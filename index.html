I am a PhD student in the FLOWERS team at Inria Bordeaux, supervised by Pierre-Yves Oudeyer and Clément Moulin-Frier. I use curiosity-driven AI to discover novel phenomena in artificial-life systems — cellular automata in particular. My broader interests sit at the intersection of machine learning, evolutionary algorithms, reinforcement learning, and cognitive science, and I am fascinated by emergence in all its forms.

Research

Complex systems — biological, ecological, computational — produce striking emergent behaviors that we still cannot reliably discover, characterize, or steer. The classical approach is hand-designed exploration: a researcher specifies what to look for, and a search procedure obliges. This scales poorly when the space of possible behaviors is large and the relevant axes of variation are unknown in advance.

I work on agents that learn what to look for. The systems I build combine autotelic reinforcement learning, generative models, and evolutionary search to autonomously propose goals, discover novel phenomena, and steer dynamical systems toward target behaviors — without the descriptors being specified up front. Recent work (under review) demonstrates closed-loop agents that generalize zero-shot to out-of-distribution control targets in continuous cellular automata, in collaboration with the Levin Lab at Tufts.

I see this line of work as an early step toward foundation models as engines of open-ended scientific discovery: agents that explore systems they have never seen before, surface non-obvious phenomena, and propose hypotheses for a human scientist to verify. My current focus is making the discovery loop more sample-efficient and more transferable across systems, with cellular automata as a tractable but unbounded testbed.
